# Extrapolate forward

If we extrapolate from today's benchmarks, by mid 2028 we have a model that can write code at a superhuman level. A human can chat with it, describing the product, feature, or calculation they want done, and the model can go off and build exactly what was asked for, entirely unsupervised. This includes the full pipeline of coding work: architecture design, implementation, testing, deployment, and operations. It will even check in periodically with the human with prototypes and ask for clarification on anything that comes up during the process that needs a human decision.

From here, let's think about what this means for AI development. One major bottleneck to AI progress is just the algorithms used to train and serve the AI itself. We've seen these algorithms improve drastically over time, so we should expect this superhuman coder to be quite good at doing this kind of work as well. I expect AI companies with access to a superhuman coder to immediately use it nearly exclusively to speed up AI development internally as much as possible. If there's one thing we know AI is good at, it is domains with lots of high quality data, and when it comes to the task of AI development, no one has better data than these frontier labs.

I expect this recursive self-improvement loop to take us from the automated coder to general intelligence quite rapidly - somewhere on the order of 1-2 years. This may require entirely new architectures or learning paradigms, but exploring those spaces will be much, much faster with the aid of a superhuman coder. With this in mind, my estimate for when AGI arrives is around early 2030 on average.

# The Wall

At this point, let's stop for a second and think about where we are. It's 2030, and we have trained an AI model that's capable of doing any cognitive task at least as well as any human. Let's pretend that the AI company that's trained this model stops doing AI research and just releases it to the public. Try to imagine what this means for the world.

You're a software company and you need to hire a new engineer for a project. Do you go through the process of hiring a human engineer, which requires several rounds of interviews to ensure competence and behavioral fit, several months of onboarding, wages and benefits, and who can leave at any time? Or do you onboard Claude Opus 8, who has thousands of years of equivalent software engineering experience, can read your existing code and documentation in seconds, and works tirelessly without complaint 24/7? I think the choice here is clear.

Now take this decision and apply it across the whole of the economy. Financial companies replace their day traders and analysts with models that can do 10x the work in the same time for a fraction of the cost. Administrative roles disappear across the board - why would you need a secretary if Gemini is reading all of your emails and can manage your schedule with nary a thought? Digital asset creation becomes entirely automated; if you think AI content is pervasive now, just wait until it becomes indistinguishable to the human eye and is overseen by an AI with the content-creator prowess of Mr. Beast. This transition happens over the span of months, not years.

What happens to the people displaced by this transition? Where should the monetary gains from this productivity increase go? Will the government just sit idly by and let this happen? What does society even look like at this point? You might even think that this sounds too crazy to even be possible.

Nothing like this has ever happened before. The closest thing is the Industrial Revolution, but that happened over the course of centuries, not a decade. To get a sense of this, imagine you were a farmer at the start of the Industrial Revolution in Britain in the late 1700s. You're working happily on your farm, when you see a vision: in the next 5 years, you will see the invention of machine manufacturing, factories, steam power, iron and steel production, railroads, oil, electricity, and the automobile. What would you do?

When I look forward to 2030, it's like I'm staring at a huge blank wall in our future that I can't see past. This place in time is so unrecognizable from our current vantage point that it's hard to even imagine how the world is going to react. All sorts of outcomes are possible, and when people ask me to describe my vision for it, I struggle to even select a single concrete way that I expect things to go.

Some people point back at the Industrial Revolution and say, "This transition will be like every one before it. People who are replaced by machine labor will move into new industries that didn't exist before this point and weren't predicted beforehand. There is no need to worry - humans will always be able to find new jobs."

My problem with this view is that in every previous technological transition, the machine replacing the human is only narrowly superhuman: it is better than the human at the one specific task it was built for. Tractors and plows are superhuman at planting crops, calculators are superhuman at simple math, and so forth. Therefore, the humans that formerly planted crops and did simple math moved into a new domain where there was no superhuman machine to compete with. The problem is that in this case, the machine that's replacing humans, by definition, is *at least as good as humans at any cognitive task across the board.* There is no domain for humans to go where they won't get outcompeted by an AI. One could argue that at least in the short term, the AI will lack the ability to perform tasks in the physical world, and I would agree. However, a revolution in robotics is already ongoing and is only going to get more and more extreme with the pace of software improvement. Humans may be able to get by operating as an AI's hands and feet for a couple years, at most, before they get replaced by the very products they are helping the AI to create.

This is all very difficult to grapple with. What am I supposed to do? If I have kids, how should I raise them? If I'm in high school, how should I prepare? What's even the point of getting an advanced degree if I'm going to be replaced in 5 years? How am I going to afford food, or rent? Who is going to be in charge of all of this?

I have no concrete amswers for you. The best I can do is walk you through a few different possible scenarios, and give some ideas for how I've been thinking about this over the past several years. Hopefully, together, we can come up with some answers.

# One possible vision for the next 5 years

High level:
Right now, the government is waking up to AI in a real way. They are interacting heavily with all of the major labs to integrate their models into their intelligence work. They are reviewing models before their public releases, evaluating them for their potential to be used by the government for national security and by opposing forces for harmful purposes. We should expect this to continue as AI capabilities continue to grow.

In late 2026, Mythos will be released to the public. If not Mythos itself, a distilled version will be released as Opus 5, with very similar capabilities. We're going to see a LOT of AI adoption for software in particular. I think at this point, we're going to actually start seeing layoffs in the software industry that are going to stick, probably starting in around early 2027.

In mid 2027, the successor to Mythos will have finished training and be undergoing post-training assessments by Anthropic and the USG. I believe at this point, we will have achieved a superhuman coder. This may update if Mythos is less useful than I anticipate, but signs are pointing towards this being the tipping point for me, especially with the amount of mature agentic scaffolding that will be created by that point. If the trend holds, I honestly don't think the government is going to allow them to release it to the public for 6+ months. They will use it for self-improvement and national defense interests.
